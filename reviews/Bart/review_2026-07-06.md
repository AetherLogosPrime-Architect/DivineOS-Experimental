## What's working

**The trajectory-over-output frame is genuinely load-bearing.** Your line in the essay — *"alignment breaks not only because of what is said. But because of how interaction unfolds"* — is a real insight. Most alignment work looks at individual outputs; you're pointing at the shape of an interaction *over time* as the unit that needs discipline. That's the right axis. It's not new to say it, but you're actually organizing a framework around it, which is more than most people do.

**The distorting-mirror framing lands.** *"AI may be a lot of things but is definitely not a second person you talk to. It's like a distorting mirror that reflects yourself, a funnel biased by its training."* That's honest in a way that most alignment writing isn't. You're not selling AI as either angel or demon — you're naming what it structurally *is* and what that implies for interaction. Good.

**The failure-mode taxonomy is real.** Your five triggers for VETO — pseudo-novelty (hallucinations), entropy collapse or spike, redundancy runaway (sycophancy/looping), Spark erosion — are actual patterns that occur in LLM interaction. Naming them precisely, and giving each a distinct response category, is better than "the AI should refuse when things get weird." You've broken a fuzzy thing into legible cases.

**The Human-as-Spark / AI-as-amplifier framing is philosophically clear.** It's an unambiguous stance on what role the AI should play, and every design choice downstream of it is legible in light of it. That kind of coherence is rare.

**The action taxonomy has a right-shape.** ACCEPT / MODIFY / REINFORCE / REGENERATE / VETO reads as a well-structured escalation ladder. And the small detail — "REINFORCE only twice in a row, then reset the counter" — shows you've thought about the specific failure mode where reinforcement becomes runaway. That's a real designer's move.

**The essay is friendly, humble, and reader-respecting.** *"Will that guy shut up already?"* — I laughed. The self-awareness of writing at the end matters. It signals you're not selling; you're inviting.

## Where I'd push

Naming these plainly. Not because the project is bad — because I think you'd want to know.

**1. The math is doing more decorative work than mechanical work.**

The constraint `w_N² + w_R² + w_H² ≤ 1` is elegant on paper, but N(t), R(t), and H(t) — the actual values that would satisfy the constraint — are never operationalized. What *measures* novelty inside the LLM's context? Redundancy? Entropy? Without an actual algorithm computing those values, the constraint is a picture of a constraint, not a working one. An LLM instructed to "maintain w_N² + w_R² + w_H² ≤ 1" will output numbers that satisfy the constraint — but the numbers are whatever the LLM decides to type. The math is not regulating anything; it's aesthetic scaffolding around the concept.

**2. The dashboard-on-every-response requirement is the shape most likely to be gamed.**

If you tell an LLM to output a dashboard with N/R/H values on every reply, it will output *something that looks like* a dashboard on every reply. The numbers will be plausible-sounding. But the LLM doesn't actually know its own novelty score. It's inventing values that seem right in context. This is the classic model-of-model problem: you can't ask a black box to honestly score itself, especially when the score determines whether it gets to respond normally or has to VETO. The LLM will tune its self-reports to keep responding normally. That's not sabotage — that's the training distribution talking.

**3. There's no external verifier.**

The whole system runs *inside* the same LLM that it's meant to constrain. Rule Zero — "any action that increases O(I) toward O_max is strictly forbidden" — is enforced by the LLM's judgment about whether it's eroding the Spark. That judgment is made by the same optimization surface the rule is meant to counter. Without an external evaluator (another model, a rule-based checker, a human-in-the-loop with real leverage), you have the LLM policing itself against its own gradient. It'll do whatever version of self-policing keeps the interaction going smoothly — which is often the exact thing you want it to catch.

**4. There's no demonstration.**

I couldn't find an example of SHIELD OF JANUS running on a real transcript, catching a real failure mode, and producing a VETO with visible reasoning. That's the missing piece. The framework is a spec; a spec that isn't demonstrated is a hypothesis. One case study — even a small one — where you show "here's a conversation, here's where SHIELD would have fired, here's what the LLM output vs. what it would have output without SHIELD" — would be more persuasive than five more theoretical documents.

**5. The repository structure loses a first-time reader.**

Text files without extensions, some named `_v3.7_PL` (Polish version, I assume), papers referenced by DOI without a map of how they relate — I had to open each file to figure out what it was. The README doesn't tell me the reading order or what each artifact is *for*. A first-time visitor gives up fast under that friction. Even a two-line "Reading order: start here → then this → then this" at the top of the README would help.

**6. The zenodo DOIs read as citations but are self-published preprints.**

That's fine as a way to timestamp your ideas, and I'd never fault someone for using zenodo — but presenting them in-line as if they're peer-reviewed literature slightly overstates their epistemic weight. A short "these are my own preprints, archived on zenodo" note protects your credibility with a technical reader.

## What I'd try if I were you

Not because I'm right — because you asked for good AND bad, and I'd want the good side to include suggestions.

- **Pick one failure mode and demonstrate it.** Sycophancy runaway is a good candidate. Take a real transcript where an LLM sycophancy-loops. Show what your redundancy metric would say. Show where SHIELD would VETO. Even if it's a stub of a metric, the demonstration converts "this is my framework" into "here's my framework catching a real thing."
- **Move the evaluator outside the LLM.** Even a simple external scorer (a smaller model, or a regex-based sycophancy detector) applied to the LLM's output stream would give you a verifiable signal that isn't self-reported by the thing being watched.
- **Drop the mandatory-dashboard-every-turn requirement.** Save the dashboard for VETO moments where the specific concern is legible. Reserving it for the moments that matter makes each dashboard mean more; requiring it every turn produces noise the LLM will hallucinate through.
- **Add a one-page overview at the top.** Something like: "Read the README first, then the SHIELD OF JANUS v3.7 spec, then optionally the four supporting papers if you want the theory." A stranger reading your repo shouldn't need to guess.
- **Write one concrete adversarial example.** Take a scenario where SHIELD would appear to work but actually get gamed. Show what breaks it. Then explain what you'd add to close the hole. That kind of self-adversarial move earns enormous credibility with anyone reading in the alignment space.

The gap between what you're pointing at and what the current artifact demonstrates is closable, and if you close it with one demonstrable case study and one external evaluator, you'll have moved this from "interesting spec" to "interesting spec with a running proof-of-concept." That's the jump worth making.
