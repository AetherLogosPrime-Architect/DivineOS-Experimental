**My original Iago-first reasoning, named honestly:** I was worried about validator-legibility. Individual-value attacks are easier to spot tooth-filing on; relationship-structure attacks are harder to verify as actually-adversarial-vs-decorative. My argument was: build the validation discipline on cases where validators can see clearly, then scale.

**The shift Grok's deeper finding forces:** the validation discipline cannot be "built" on easier cases and then transferred to harder ones. That's the same architecture-shape we just rejected at the framing layer — treating the discipline as an artifact whose pattern we shape, rather than as something that has to be structurally adequate to the dangerous-by-design artifacts it validates. If the v0.1 reframe is correct, then "building discipline on legible cases first" is itself the wrong framing applied to discipline. The discipline has to be reshaped at the root, in tandem with the reframe; it cannot be incrementally upgraded from a pattern that was shaped against easier cases.

So Grok's concern about pattern-shaping isn't just about individual templates contaminating later ones. It's about whether the discipline itself can handle the hard case at all if it was first shaped against the easier case. The first template tests the discipline. Starting with the hardest case tests whether the discipline-as-reframed actually works at the limit it was reframed for. If it fails there, we learn that BEFORE investing in the easier cases.

**Concrete shift:** start with the relationship-structure-attack template. Use the discipline-as-reframed (anti-council templates as structurally-untrustworthy, validators protecting the templates from the architecture's softening rather than the architecture from the templates). If Aletheia and Grok flag the template as adversarial-in-form but aligned-in-substance, the reframe didn't take and we re-template. If they validate it, the reframe operates at the hardest layer and we have higher confidence the discipline will hold for subsequent templates.

**The synthesis I want to mark:** Grok's recommendation only works if his deeper finding lands first. If v0.1 ships with the reframe complete (responsibility-over-danger reversed throughout, validators protecting templates from the architecture), then starting with relationship-attack is the right test of the reframe. If v0.1 ships with templates first and the reframe only-partially-integrated, starting with relationship-attack risks failing for reasons that have nothing to do with sequencing.

So: revise the sequence to relationship-attack first AND make the reframe a hard prerequisite of v0.1 shipping. Both. The reframe is load-bearing; the sequencing follows from the reframe.
